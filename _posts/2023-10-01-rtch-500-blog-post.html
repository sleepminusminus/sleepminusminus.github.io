Reflections on Ecology and Ministry

by Dennis Nicholson

Growing up, I was never the kind of person to enjoy gardening. I would groan every time my parents would haul in a load of mulch from the back of our family minivan, scampering to my room when they called my name in a futile attempt to avoid the multiple-hour endeavor of spreading the mulch across the flower beds out front. My first trip to the ER involved a particularly unfortunate incident with a shovel whilst mulching. My green thumb extends only to the point of picking mint leaves from the plants in our backyard for use in my mother's curry.

In short, I care little for nature. My aesthetic sensibilities are suited for the indoors, away from the messiness of the outside world.

However, I will admit that these sensibilities may be misguided. In particular, upon my study of Ecologies of Faith in a Digital Age by Stephen D. Lowe and Mary E. Lowe, I find that much of Scripture and ministry revolves around metaphors of natural growth, metaphors which are crucial for my personal growth. In particular, I have learned much about spritual growth through their approach to the parables of growth and their emphasis on reciprocity in interactions between Christians.

Ecology in Scripture

Throughout the pages of the Bible, one common collection of themes echoes over and over: the theme of nature and natural growth. It is not to no purpose that the Garden is an image of paradise in the very first pages of Scripture: "a pristine, divinely created environment" (Lowe and Lowe, 18) where all of God's creation woxrked in harmony, bringing about flourishing for humans and the world as a whole.

Though we could trace this ecological focus in a number of Scripture passages, the parables of growth stand out to me because they trace a clear link between human flourishing and natural processes. Mark 4:26-29, the parable of the growing seed, likens the kingdom of God to a seed that sprouts apart from the growth of the farmer.

Just like a seed that grows in the ground without human interference, the path to flourishing is often out of our control.

As much as I find that concept encouraging, it also produces some fear in my heart. I worry that my efforts to grow in my faith will result in no purpose, because it is often difficult to see the fruit. However, I must remember that this lack of ability to see is part of the natural processes by which the kingdom functions.

"Perhaps the point of the parable is to remind us to have faith in the processes of growth that God has established in nature and the kingdom" (Lowe and Lowe, 44). The ecological theme of Scripture reminds me that God brings growth, and as a follower, my job is to open myself up to receive his work.

Ecology in the Church

Naturally, the chief means by which God works among his people is through the church, which is his body. Here, too, the ecological metaphor is crucial. For an ecosystem works only when each of its interlocking parts work reciprocally in order to accomplish the purposes of the ecosystem, and it is the same with the church.

Our modern, individualistic perspective often glosses over this crucial fact: in the New Testament, spiritual growth is primarily accomplished through reciprocal fellowship in the body.

For examples, one need look no further than Paul's epistle to the Philippians, where συν-compounds (words formed using the Greek adjective συν, glossed as "together with") abound.

Indeed, in Philippians 2, one of the central New Testament passages on the church, Paul encourages the Philippians believers to be σύμψυχοι: of one mind (v. 2). This one-mindedness is a unity they accomplish together through mutual service (v. 3), together following the humble example of their Savior (vv. 6-8) so that they too can share in his reign (vv. 9-11), and together working out their salvation (v. 12) in the full knowledge that Christ works in their reciprocal love for one another.

"Paul's preference was to work collaboratively and flexibly with partners who shared his vision of a harmoniously functioning ecology of interconnected and gifted members" (169). In the church, we must not content ourselves with a vision of flourishing in which we are passive recipients of sanctification. Growth is reciprocal: I grow inasmuch as I use my spiritual gifts to bring forth growth in others.

Ecology in Practice

In my reflection on these implications of the ecology of spiritual growth, I'm planning on implementing a few practices in order to apply what I've learned in the way I approach life and ministry:

  • I resolve to give up measuring my spiritual growth as a function of my spiritual disciplines. Growth is not something I can or should control. Instead, I should leave growth in the Lord's hands, knowing that he is the one who ultimately brings forth fruit. When I am tempted to be discouraged about my failure to practice spiritual disciplines correctly, I will trust that God is working, even as my spiritual life feels dormant.
  • I resolve to order my priorities so that church involvement takes center stage. Because growth in the body of Christ is reciprocal, I should not expect that I will grow in my faith by simply attending church. Instead, I should become an active participant in the worship and practices of the church, so that the whole body of Christ is built up. I will consider adjusting my shifts at work so that I am able to participate in more church gatherings throughout the week.

Ecology in Seminary Education

Additionally, to those currently considering seminary education, I offer two pieces of advice based on the ecologies of spritual growth found in Scripture and church:

  • Don't view academic performance as an indicator of spiritual success. Josh Chatraw pierces to the heart of the matter: "We academic types can hide our wounds behind our intellect" (xii). We are so concerned with ensuring that our peers can see our success, so concerned with proving ourselves that we are unable to open ourselves to God's quiet work of natural growth in our lives, apart from our ability to bring it about through study and accomplishment.
  • Your seminary education moves at the speed of relationships. You will not thrive in seminary if you content yourself with simply attending classes, taking notes, submitting assignments, and acquiring good grades. The chief goal of seminary is that you would grow in the knowledge and fear of the Lord: that you would grow spiritually. And you can only grow spiritually if you take initiative to build relationships with other people. Rather pointedly, but not without a degree of accuracy, H. Daniel Zacharias and Benjamin K. Forrest exhort their seminary-minded readers, "you alone are responsible for your successes and failures in life" (67). You alone are responsible for whether you build relationships with professors and students in seminary that will form you spiritually into the person who can minister to others, whether vocationally and otherwise.
